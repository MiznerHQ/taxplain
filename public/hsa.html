What is HSA (Health Savings Account)?

Tax-Advantaged Account

Written by the TaxPlain Editorial Team · Reviewed for accuracy · Last updated July 2026

⚠️ Educational only. TaxPlain does not provide tax, legal, or financial advice. Always consult a qualified tax professional about your specific situation.
What it is

An HSA (Health Savings Account) is a triple-tax-advantaged account for people enrolled in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP). Contributions go in tax-free, the balance grows tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses come out tax-free — no other account type offers all three.

Unlike an FSA, HSA funds never expire and the account belongs to you even if you change jobs or health plans. It's also different from a 401(k) or IRA: those only give you one or two of the three tax benefits, not all three at once, which is why financial advisors often call the HSA the most efficient account available for eligible individuals.

2026 HSA contribution limits
Individual coverage
$4,400
Family coverage
$8,750

If you're 55 or older, you can add a $1,000 catch-up contribution on top of either limit. One detail people frequently overlook: these limits include employer contributions and any employer match — not just what you personally deposit. If your employer puts in $1,000, your own contribution room drops by that same amount.

Who it affects & common mistakes
👤 Who it affects

People enrolled in a qualifying High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) — for 2026, that means a minimum deductible of $1,700 for individual coverage or $3,400 for family coverage. Self-employed individuals qualify too, as long as they carry a qualifying HDHP; the account isn't tied to employer sponsorship the way an FSA is.

⚠️ Common mistake

Using HSA funds for non-medical expenses before age 65. Early non-qualified withdrawals trigger both ordinary income tax and a 20% penalty.

HSA vs FSA — can you have both?

The two accounts get confused constantly, and for good reason: both let you set aside pre-tax money for medical costs. The difference is portability. An FSA is tied to your employer and typically has a "use it or lose it" rule each year, while an HSA rolls over indefinitely and stays with you.

Generally, you can't contribute to both a standard health FSA and an HSA in the same year — enrolling in one usually disqualifies you from the other. The one exception is a limited-purpose FSA, which only covers dental and vision costs and can be paired with an HSA.

HSA eligible expenses

Per IRS Publication 502, qualified expenses cover a wide range of medical, dental, and vision costs:

  • Doctor, dentist, and specialist visits
  • Prescription medications
  • Vision care, including glasses and contacts
  • Certain over-the-counter medications and supplies
  • Physical therapy and chiropractic care

A common point of confusion: gym memberships generally do not qualify on their own. They can only be reimbursed if a doctor prescribes the membership to treat a specific diagnosed condition, with documentation on file — a general wellness membership doesn't count.

HSA rules after age 65

Turning 65 changes what you can do with HSA funds. The 20% penalty for non-medical withdrawals disappears entirely — after 65, you can withdraw for any purpose. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses stay completely tax-free, exactly as before.

Withdrawals for non-medical reasons after 65 are taxed as ordinary income, similar to how a traditional IRA works. This makes an HSA function as a backup retirement account once you no longer need to worry about medical-only withdrawal rules.

Example: Say you withdraw $5,000 after turning 65. If it goes toward a qualified medical expense, you owe nothing. If you use that same $5,000 for something unrelated — like a home repair — you'll owe ordinary income tax on it, but you avoid the 20% penalty that would have applied before age 65.

What to do right now
Max out your HSA contributions every year if you can afford to — it's the only truly triple-tax-advantaged account available. Consider investing the funds rather than leaving them in cash, since the account is designed for long-term, tax-free growth. If you also itemize, review your itemized deductions alongside your HSA strategy, and confirm with a tax professional that you're within the current-year contribution limit.
Frequently asked questions
What are the HSA contribution limits for 2026?
For 2026, the IRS HSA contribution limit is $4,400 for individual coverage and $8,750 for family coverage. If you're 55 or older, you can contribute an additional $1,000 catch-up amount. These limits include any contributions your employer makes on your behalf, so add employer contributions to your own before checking you haven't gone over.
Can I have both an HSA and an FSA?
Generally no, not at the same time, if the FSA is a standard health FSA — having one usually disqualifies you from contributing to an HSA. The exception is a limited-purpose FSA (covering only dental and vision expenses), which can be paired with an HSA.
What counts as an HSA eligible expense?
IRS Publication 502 lists qualified medical expenses, which include doctor visits, prescriptions, dental and vision care, and certain over-the-counter items. Some gym memberships and fitness programs can qualify only if prescribed by a doctor to treat a specific medical condition — a general gym membership for wellness typically does not qualify.
What happens to my HSA after I turn 65?
After age 65, you can withdraw HSA funds for any purpose, not just medical expenses, without the usual 20% penalty. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses remain completely tax-free. Withdrawals for non-medical purposes are taxed as ordinary income, similar to a traditional IRA.
Can self-employed people contribute to an HSA?
Yes. Unlike an FSA, an HSA is not tied to employer sponsorship. Self-employed individuals can open and contribute to an HSA as long as they're enrolled in a qualifying high-deductible health plan, and can typically deduct contributions directly on their tax return.
